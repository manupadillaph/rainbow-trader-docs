    Globally, S2, F1 Macro, and Entry F1 do not translate into realized PnL. What does stand out is the extreme negative Trades vs PnL relationship, which means the monetization problem is dominated by turnover and friction rather than by the absence of predictive signal.

    Insights

    Normalizing by trade count softens the raw S2 -> PnL disconnect, but the effect remains modest and fold-dependent. This supports a narrower claim than 'friction disappears': controlling for turnover reveals some per-trade edge, but not enough to rescue high-activity models automatically.

    2.3 The Friction Mechanism (Data-Driven)

    The mechanism is:

    1. A model with better S2/F1 detects more patterns → generates more trade signals
    2. More signals → more trades (the gate lets more through)

    3. Each trade costs friction (slippage + commission round-trip)

    4. Profitable trials average 106 trades

    5. Losing trials average 239 trades
    6. Trade difference: 133 extra trades
    7. Average friction per trade: $2.80
    8. Estimated friction drag from extra trades: $372

    When PnL is normalized per trade (removing the trade-count effect), positive correlations emerge — confirming that better models DO capture slightly more edge per trade, but not enough to offset the friction from trading more often.

    Friction Paradox Insight: The global S2-vs-PnL correlation is r=-0.01 (effectively zero), while Trades-vs-PnL is r=-0.81 (EXTREME negative). This is the core friction paradox: signal quality alone does not predict profitability because more signal → more trades → more friction. Implication for rescue: models must be MORE SELECTIVE (higher threshold, stricter gate) to reduce trade count and minimize friction drag.

    🔍 Rescue Analysis: The $2.80/trade friction × 133 extra trades gap = $372 explains why the 1.2% profitability rate is structural, not accidental. Rescue targeting: keeping trade count ≤150 (profitable average = 106 trades) would save ~$250 in friction vs the losing average. Per-trade edge normalization shows models DO learn slightly better with higher S2, but the marginal gain (~$0.50/trade) cannot offset $2.80/trade friction — breakeven requires 5.6× improvement in per-trade edge, which no current model achieves. This sets the rescue design constraint: optimize for fewer, higher-quality trades rather than more accurate signal detection.

    🔍 Rescue Analysis: AXV stands out: lowest gap (0.045) but strongest r(Gap,PnL) = –0.63, meaning even small overfitting is very costly for AXV — the rescue should enforce strict regularization (higher dropout, lower LR) for AXV experiments specifically. Per-model regularization priors should be encoded in the rescue search space bounds.

    Insights

    The bucket table makes the trade-off concrete. The low-trade bucket has the least negative average PnL, but the medium bucket has better average PnL/trade, Sharpe, and Win%. For the rescue, that means we should target controlled turnover rather than blindly minimizing trade count.

    🔍 Rescue Analysis: The rescue target zone is the Low-Medium boundary (100–150 trades): this captures the Low bucket's PnL advantage (least negative at –$419 avg) while approaching the Medium bucket's better per-trade efficiency. The High bucket (>297 trades, –$1,012 avg PnL, $1,048 avg friction) is structurally unprofitable and should be avoided. For neighbor generation: when perturbing seed hyperparameters, evaluate whether the perturbation pushes estimated trade count toward 100–150 or away from it. The $762 avg friction gap between Low ($286) and High ($1,048) buckets = $762 of pure execution waste.

    🔍 Rescue Analysis: No model has positive r(S2,PnL), confirming multi-metric seed selection is mandatory — optimizing S2 alone helps NO architecture. LOG has the most extreme r(Trades,PnL) = –0.88, making it the most friction-sensitive model — LOG rescue MUST prioritize trade-count reduction above all else. AXV has r(EntryF1,PnL) = –0.61 (strongest negative in any cell) — AXV models with high timing skill are the most friction-trapped. The extraction script should weight PnL/Sharpe seeds more heavily for LOG and AXV, while S2/Entry F1 seeds may be more useful for TCN/VIT where the execution gap is less extreme.

    12. Key Takeaways for Tier B Rescue Design

    Data-driven synthesis from 1183 trials (≥5 trades) out of 1185 total completed:

    The Friction Paradox (CRITICAL)

    1. S2 vs PnL: r=-0.01 — Signal quality does NOT predict profitability
    2. Trades vs PnL: r=-0.81 — More trades STRONGLY predicts losses
    3. Profitable trials average 106 trades vs 239 for losing (Δ=133 trades)
    4. Only 1.2% of trials are profitable — selectivity is the key differentiator

    Overfitting Dynamics

    1. Feature count vs S1-S2 gap: r=+0.38 — More features = more overfitting risk
    2. 45 forced features require aggressive regularization to prevent memorization
    3. Weight_decay shows surprising positive correlation with gap (r=+0.21)

    Rescue Configuration Recommendations

    1. Test selectivity levers explicitly: higher prediction thresholds and entry-gate neighborhoods
    2. Entry gate weight vs Trades: r=-0.01, vs PnL: r=+0.00
    3. Keep regularization model-specific: search broader dropout/weight_decay, but avoid one blanket bias
    4. Increase epoch budget selectively: favor families or runs that actually hit the budget ceiling
    5. Seed diversity (CONFIRMED): 5 metrics × 4 top = 20 seeds per model before dedup. Metrics: S3, S2, PnL, Sharpe, Entry F1 (see Section 6.4 for redundancy analysis). Cross-fold seeding enabled, cross-model seeding disabled
