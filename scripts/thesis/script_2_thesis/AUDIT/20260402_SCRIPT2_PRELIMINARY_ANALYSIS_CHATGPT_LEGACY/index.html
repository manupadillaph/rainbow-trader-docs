    Rainbow-Trader Thesis Review and Decision Path

    LEGACY STATUS (2026-05-16): Frozen because the Script 2 report generators changed after this manual analysis was written. The regenerated reports now use updated metrics, corrected trial ordering, and refreshed selection logic, so conclusions in this file are not valid for new thesis decisions. Use this file only as historical provenance. Links inside this file may still point to pre-freeze canonical names. LEGACY NOTE (2026-05-15): The ensemble and PnL+Fric/friction-proxy conclusions in this preliminary document are legacy. Future Thesis 2 ensemble work must use real closed-trade/session economics and the redesigned friction-pressure criterion.

    What This Document Is

    Critical external review of the Rainbow Trader thesis structure and the strength of its evidence. Cleanly separates what the results support (Claim A: models learn signal above random baselines — partially supported) from what they do not yet support (Claim B: signal converts to economic profit — not shown).

    Identifies the main validity threats: objective–profit mismatch (Optuna optimizes S2, not PnL), data-snooping risk across multiple model evaluations, and time-series leakage risk despite walk-forward. References formal statistical guardrails: White's Reality Check, Hansen's SPA, Bailey & López de Prado's Deflated Sharpe Ratio and Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO). The validity threat framework remains fully applicable to current thesis decisions and ensemble evidence. Consult this before making any claim about model performance or edge.

    Context, scope, and what the thesis claims

    Your system is positioned as a research → training → execution pipeline for quantitative trading, with separate modules for data fetching/processing, label generation, window preparation, model training, bot decision logic, backtesting, and live execution. citeturn26view2turn26view1

    The thesis statement in your experiment plan is: exploitable patterns exist in real market data, and the system can detect and exploit them to achieve positive economic edge over random baselines. citeturn20view4

    Your methodology is structured in two scripts:

    Script 1 is a sanity and leakage check suite using controlled data sources (dummy and random walk), plus two “controlled leak” mechanisms (a leak indicator and a label-copy signal). The purpose is to verify: (a) patterns are found when present, (b) patterns are not found on random data, and (c) the leak and label-copy controls behave as expected. citeturn25view0turn10view1turn11view0

    Script 2 is the thesis test on real market data, with (i) progressive model complexity (rule-based → logistic regression → MLP → TCN → ViT → Axial ViT), (ii) multiple baseline models (always-none plus three calibrated random baselines), (iii) walk-forward evaluation using 4 folds (3 months train + 1 month test), and (iv) multiple-comparisons-aware statistics across trial distributions rather than “best trial” cherry-picks. citeturn25view0turn8view0turn22view0turn20view2

    A core design choice is that your optimization target (objective_value) is a 3-stage weighted objective that mixes ML metrics on training and validation periods with a trading-simulation stage that incorporates return, Sharpe, win rate, and an “alpha adjustment.” citeturn25view0turn22view0

    What the results support and what they do not

    Your “statistical tests” report states: 101 experiments and 1,675 completed trials were analyzed, and the main hypothesis test compares distributions of objective_value from trained models vs distributions from random baselines using a two-sided Mann–Whitney U test with Holm–Sidak correction and a rank-biserial effect-size threshold (> 0.3) as a pass criterion. citeturn8view0

    On that target metric, multiple model families pass at the aggregated (“grouped by model”) level.

    For example, grouped-by-model medians show:

    • Logistic regression: median objective_value 0.2048 vs 0.1831 (random_l), p_corr ≈ 6.49e-07, effect ≈ 0.303; it also passes vs the other two random types. citeturn8view0
    • MLP and MLP_EX: larger median gaps and effect sizes (effect values ≈ 0.51–0.66 across random types). citeturn8view0
    • TCN and ViT: pass vs all three random baselines with p_corr values well below 0.05 and effect sizes above 0.33. citeturn8view0

    So, on the proxy metric you defined, “trained trial distributions tend to rank above calibrated-random trial distributions” is supported in your current analysis design. citeturn8view0turn6view6

    Your preliminary analysis also records a second point: this proxy superiority has not translated into consistent or average profitability once fees and execution frictions are included in the backtests. It reports (a) only 1.2% of trials are profitable and (b) correlation between an internal signal score and PnL is near zero (r ≈ -0.01). citeturn6view5

    It also reports that PnL is dominated by trade count (“friction paradox” framing): r(Trades, PnL) ≈ -0.81, and all profitable trials are in the low-trade region (≤228 trades), with profitable trials averaging 106 trades vs 239 for losing trials. citeturn6view5

    A third result in the same document is fold dependence: all profitable trials are in folds F1 and F3, while folds F2 and F4 have zero profitable trials in that run set. citeturn6view5

    There is also an internal consistency issue to fix before you treat your docs as a “paper-ready” record: one section states that current experiments use a 4h timeframe, while your experiment plan for Script 1 and Script 2 lays out a 5m timeframe, and the same section later references multi-timeframe expansions that include 5m. That mismatch signals document drift or a naming inconsistency that will matter in peer review and in investor diligence. citeturn23view2turn25view0turn20view2

    Thesis validity with critique of the current evidence

    Your thesis has two linked claims:

    Claim A: “Models can learn signal that is not present in calibrated random baselines.”
    Claim B: “That signal can be exploited for economic profit after costs, in a repeatable way.”

    Your current evidence base supports Claim A under your defined objective_value and your current statistical framework. citeturn8view0turn6view6

    Your current evidence base does not support Claim B yet, and your own analysis states this directly (negative average PnL for both distributions; low profitable-trial rate). citeturn7view0turn6view5

    That split is not a failure; it is a decision point: your next work should treat “signal → profit conversion” as the main research problem, not “signal existence.”

    The main threats to validity I would flag, and why they matter:

    The objective–profit mismatch
    Your optimization target is a weighted blend of ML metrics and backtest composites. citeturn22view0
    Your own results show near-zero correlation between the internal score and PnL. citeturn6view5
    That means you can win the statistical test while still losing money, because the optimizer is rewarded for properties that are not aligned with net returns after costs. This is not rare in ML-for-trading, and it becomes more likely as you add features and model capacity.

    Data snooping and multiple testing remains a structural risk
    You are running many trials and comparing many configurations. Your framework already recognizes the issue (distribution-level testing, Holm–Sidak correction, and plans for DSR / White RC / Hansen SPA / PBO). citeturn8view0turn6view4
    In the research literature, repeated strategy search on the same dataset can create false discoveries unless you adjust claims for the search process itself. White’s Reality Check formalizes this risk for model searches. citeturn12search2
    Hansen’s SPA test is designed for “does any model beat the benchmark?” under multiple comparisons with improved power properties. citeturn12search3
    Bailey & López de Prado’s Deflated Sharpe Ratio addresses selection bias and non-normality when you search across many variants. citeturn14search2
    Their “Probability of Backtest Overfitting” provides a way to estimate the chance that the in-sample winner fails out-of-sample. citeturn14search30
    Harvey’s “Backtesting” work frames multiple-testing haircuts as a necessity in finance research claims. citeturn14search3

    Time-series leakage risks remain even with walk-forward folds
    Your Script 1 validation is designed to detect leakage with random data and controlled leak signals. citeturn25view0turn10view1turn11view0
    That is a relevant guardrail, but it does not replace leak-resistant cross-validation designs for labels that depend on future events. In financial ML, standard k-fold can leak label-horizon overlap, and purging and embargo are used to reduce that risk. This is a core theme in López de Prado’s work and in his failure-mode catalog for ML funds. citeturn14search23turn14search24

    Regime dependence is not an “edge,” it is a constraint
    Your fold outcomes show “profit exists in some folds and not in others.” citeturn6view5
    This matches the broader idea that market efficiency can vary across time and conditions (Adaptive Markets view). citeturn12search5
    The practical implication is: your system needs regime gating or fast adaptation, or it will keep generating “profit in pockets, loss elsewhere.”

    Transaction costs and turnover can erase small edges
    Your results show that fewer trades aligns with improved profit outcomes, which is consistent with the idea that costs scale with trading activity. citeturn6view5
    In microstructure terms, spreads, fees, and price impact enter as an ongoing tax on turnover. citeturn19search2
    At a macro level, the cost of active investing can be large even in liquid equity markets; this is a reminder that “finding alpha” and “keeping alpha after costs” are different problems. citeturn19search0

    A critique of one of your own inferences
    The preliminary analysis states “minimum complexity to beat random is logistic regression” and uses that to infer linear structure. citeturn6view5
    But in your tier × model tables, Tier A × logistic fails vs all three random types in that breakdown. citeturn8view0turn20view2
    So “logistic beats random” is supported in pooled results, but “logistic beats random under the Tier A feature set” is not supported by the tier-specific breakdown shown in the report snippet. This matters because it changes the story from “linear signal in close+RSI” to “linear signal emerges with a richer feature set,” which is a different claim.

    What I would do next and what could go wrong with each step

    I would treat your next phase as three parallel tracks with one gate: no new feature tiers until the economic-evaluation gate is stable.

    Economic-evaluation gate
    Define one “locked” evaluation protocol and one final holdout segment that you do not use for model selection. The goal is to reduce the feedback loop that creates data snooping. This aligns with the multiple-testing literature: once you search across many variants, you need a protocol that distinguishes exploration from claims. citeturn12search2turn14search3turn14search2
    Failure mode: the holdout becomes a second training set because you look at it too often. The fix is process, not code.

    Signal-to-profit conversion as the primary objective
    Your own analysis says profitability is governed by trade count and that tightening thresholds improved PnL in 48/60 reruns. citeturn7view0turn7view3
    So I would prioritize: calibrating probabilities, making abstention a first-class action, and tuning execution thresholds per fold or per regime, before adding more features.
    Failure mode: you can tune thresholds in-sample and recreate the overfitting problem at the “policy layer.” That is why you need the holdout gate and PBO-style checks. citeturn14search30

    Ensemble gating to reduce low-conviction trades
    Your own plan proposes ensembles as a selectivity mechanism. citeturn7view1
    The general ML literature on time-series classification includes strong ensemble baselines (for example, InceptionTime is explicitly an ensemble). citeturn13search9
    In finance, combining model families is also used in published work for predictive tasks. citeturn13search0
    Failure mode: ensembles can reduce recall and can suppress the small set of trades that carry PnL if your signal is sparse. The fix is to measure “profit per trade” and “profit per unit turnover” and not only accuracy-style scores.

    Model choice for compute constraints
    Given your compute pressure, I would add at least one fast baseline family that is designed for time-series classification with low training cost. MiniROCKET is positioned as a fast transform + linear classifier approach with high speed and competitive accuracy in TSC benchmarks. citeturn13search2
    Failure mode: benchmark success on UCR-style datasets does not guarantee transfer to market data. The value here is iteration speed: it helps you test label design, windowing, and leakage controls at lower cost.

    Regime-aware trading and regime-aware learning
    Your fold pattern (profit in F1/F3, none in F2/F4) is a trigger for regime gating or mixture-of-experts. citeturn6view5turn7view2
    Regime-switching models for crypto volatility and HMM-based approaches are present in the literature. citeturn15search7turn15search3
    Failure mode: regime labels become another feature set that can leak if built using future windows, or they become another knob to overfit. The fix is to build regimes using only past information at each timestamp and to evaluate gating out-of-sample.

    Alternative data, but staged by “cost and latency”
    Your plan lists sentiment, on-chain, and derivatives features. citeturn7view1
    Evidence exists that some sentiment proxies have out-of-sample predictive use in crypto return forecasting (example: Fear & Greed Index forecasting horizons in published work). citeturn15search6
    On-chain features are also used in published direction-prediction studies. citeturn15search1
    Derivatives features can encode positioning and carry-type signals; BIS work discusses crypto futures and funding-rate economics in the context of carry. citeturn15search30
    Failure mode: alternative data creates pipeline fragility (missingness, timestamp alignment errors) and can create “soft leakage” via delayed publication times. The fix is to treat time alignment as part of the experiment spec and to log “feature availability at decision time.”

    Multi-timeframe and multi-symbol tiers after the gate
    Your Tier D/E plan is a large feature expansion (multi-timeframe and multi-symbol). citeturn7view2turn20view4
    Architectures like PatchTST and iTransformer are proposed for multivariate time series tasks with large lookback windows and many variables. citeturn13search3turn24search1
    Failure mode: feature count expansion increases the hyperparameter search space and raises overfitting risk, so your statistical corrections and “holdout gate” become more necessary, not less. citeturn14search2turn12search2

    Self-supervised or foundation-model leverage as a compute strategy
    Pretrained time-series models exist (example: TimesFM from entity["company","Google Research","machine learning lab"]) and are distributed with code and checkpoints. citeturn24search3turn24search7
    Failure mode: these models target forecasting tasks, while your problem mixes classification, policy thresholds, and execution constraints. The benefit may come from using pretrained embeddings as features, not from end-to-end trading optimization.
