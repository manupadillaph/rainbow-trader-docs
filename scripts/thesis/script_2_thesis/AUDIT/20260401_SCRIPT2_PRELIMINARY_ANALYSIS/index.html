    1. Executive Summary

    This document consolidates all quantitative findings from Script 2 through Tier B (1,185 completed trainable trials across 72 experiments and 4 runs) into a preliminary analysis. It synthesizes the existing audit, correlation analysis, experiment results, and thesis methodology into a single reference.

    Key Findings

    1. Models beat random baselines on signal quality — Trained models achieve S2 (validation score) in the 0.24–0.30 range vs 0.19–0.25 for randoms. Objective scores similarly exceed random by 0.1–0.2 points.

    2. Models fail to consistently translate signal quality into profitability — Only 14 out of 1,185 trials (1.2%) achieve positive net PnL. All 14 profitable trials have ≤200 trades. No random baseline trial has ever achieved positive PnL.

    3. The Friction Paradox — The correlation between S2 (validation quality) and PnL (profit) is r = −0.01 (effectively zero), while the correlation between trade count and PnL is r = −0.81 (extremely strong negative). This means selectivity, not signal quality, is the primary determinant of profitability in the current framework.

    4. Two distinct clusters exist — A signal-quality cluster (S2, Entry F1, F1 Macro) and an execution-economics cluster (PnL, Sharpe, Win%) that are structurally misaligned. Improving one does not automatically improve the other.

    5. Window size has weak global impact (r(WS,S2) = +0.17) but strong per-model effects — TCN shows r(WS,S2) = +0.49, while Logistic shows r = −0.06.

    6. More features increase overfitting more than signal quality — The 45-feature Tier B regime buys +0.019 S2 at the cost of +0.228 S1-S2 gap — a 12:1 overfitting-to-signal ratio.

    7. The thesis validation methodology compares distributions of all trial objectives (not just best-vs-best), using Mann-Whitney U tests, effect sizes, Bootstrap CI, and Deflated Sharpe Ratio to avoid selection bias.

    8. Formal statistical validation confirms the edge — Mann-Whitney U tests with Holm-Sidak correction show that 5 of 7 architectures statistically outperform all 3 random baselines (p < 0.05, effect size > 0.3). Both tiers pass all tests. Effect sizes increase with model complexity (logistic 0.34 → mlp_ex 0.66), and with feature count (Tier A avg effect 0.34 → Tier B avg effect 0.52). See Section 12 and the Statistical Tests Annexo.

    9. Research-backed improvement potential is substantial — Current experiments use only price-derived features from a single timeframe on one symbol. At least 8 independently documented improvement avenues — each with peer-reviewed support — remain unexplored: model ensembles, sentiment analysis, on-chain metrics, derivatives data, multi-timeframe features, multi-symbol context, market regime detection, and advanced architectures. See Section 14.

    4. The Friction Paradox — Signal Quality vs Profitability

    4.1 The Core Disconnect

    From the correlation analysis across 1,183 trials (≥5 trades):

    Correlation r Interpretation
    S2 vs PnL -0.01 ZERO — Signal quality does NOT predict profitability
    Trades vs PnL -0.81 EXTREME — More trades strongly predicts losses
    S2 vs F1 Macro +0.85 Very high — both measure signal quality
    Trades vs Sharpe +0.45 Strong — more trades, better per-trade metrics
    S2 vs Sharpe +0.01 ZERO

    4.2 Two-Cluster Framework

    The correlation structure reveals two distinct, nearly independent clusters:

    Signal-Quality Cluster:

    • S2 (validation generalization)
    • F1 Macro (directional accuracy)
    • Entry F1 (trade timing)
    • These correlate highly with each other (r = +0.57 to +0.85) but NOT with PnL

    Execution-Economics Cluster:

    • PnL (net profit)
    • Sharpe (risk-adjusted return)
    • Win% (success rate)
    • Trade count (selectivity)
    • PnL dominated by trade count (r = -0.81)

    The bridge between clusters is selectivity: profitable trials average 106 trades vs 239 for losers — a difference of 133 trades. At an estimated $2.80 per trade in friction, this gap represents $372 in friction drag.

    4.3 Trade-Level Evidence

    Direct inspection of trade-level data from database/rainbow_trader.sqlite confirms:

    Trial Model/Fold S2 Trades TP Exits SL Exits PnL
    T3698 TCN-F2 0.339 (best S2 in audit) 311 87 224 -$701
    T2530 TCN-F3 0.257 17 8 9 +$48
    T2662 VIT-F1 0.296 275 94 181 -$247
    T1525 VIT-F1 0.255 40 23 17 +$82
    • T3698 has the best S2 in the entire audit (0.339) yet loses $701 across 311 trades (224 stop-loss exits)
    • T2530 has lower S2 (0.257) but is profitable with only 17 trades — remains profitable despite more SL than TP exits because winning trades are larger
    • T1525 has lower S2 than T2662 (0.255 vs 0.296) but better economics: 40 trades, 57.5% win rate, +$82

    4.4 Updated Interpretation

    The failure mode is NOT "garbage trades everywhere" — trade metadata shows reasonable entry confidence and quality scores even for losing trials. The failure mode is:

    The model expands the accepted trade set faster than it improves the profitability of that marginal trade set.

    Higher S2 means the model identified more "valid-looking" opportunities, but the newly accepted trades do not satisfy the minimum win/loss mix implied by the actual payoff distribution. Each trial has an implicit break-even win rate determined by its realized TP/SL payoff asymmetry. High-S2 high-trade-count trials fail that break-even condition; low-trade-count trials clear it through selectivity.

    5. Window Size Impact

    5.1 Global Correlations

    Across all 1,183 trials with ≥5 trades:

    Correlation r Interpretation
    WS vs S2 +0.17 Weak positive
    WS vs Objective +0.04 ZERO
    WS vs PnL +0.02 ZERO
    WS vs Trades -0.12 Weak negative
    WS vs S1-S2 Gap +0.08 ZERO
    WS vs Sharpe +0.08 ZERO

    Global deduction: Window size has a weak positive relationship with validation quality (r = +0.17) but no meaningful relationship with profitability. Larger windows do not help or hurt economically at the aggregate level.

    5.2 Per-Model Window Size Sensitivity

    Model r(WS, S2) r(WS, PnL) r(WS, Obj) n
    TCN +0.49 +0.14 +0.04 103
    VIT +0.11 -0.03 +0.07 235
    MLP +0.08 +0.02 -0.06 289
    MEX +0.07 +0.12 -0.06 216
    AXV +0.38 -0.03 +0.17 32
    LOG -0.06 +0.01 -0.01 308

    TCN is the clear window-size beneficiary. With r(WS, S2) = +0.49 (strong positive), TCN appears to genuinely benefit from longer temporal context. This aligns with TCN's architecture: larger kernel sizes and more layers have larger receptive fields, and bigger windows provide more temporal structure to exploit.

    Logistic is window-size neutral (r = -0.06), which makes sense — a linear classifier cannot extract complex temporal patterns regardless of window length.

    AXV shows moderate sensitivity (r = +0.38) but with only 32 trials, this estimate is noisy.

    5.3 Deduction

    Window size is an architecture-specific lever, not a universal one. For the rescue design:

    • TCN: search wider window ranges, larger windows genuinely help S2
    • VIT/MLP/MEX: window size can remain in the default search space without special emphasis
    • Logistic: window size is irrelevant to performance

    11. Statistical Methodology for Thesis Validation

    This section summarizes the methodology specified in the thesis experiment plan (docs/plans/EXPERIMENT_PLAN_THESIS_VALIDATION.md) and supporting research documents for how models will be rigorously compared against random baselines.

    11.1 Core Principle: Distribution Comparison, Not Best-vs-Best

    The thesis does NOT compare the best trained model trial against the best random trial. This would suffer from selection bias (White 2000, Harvey et al. 2016).

    Instead, statistical significance is established by comparing distributions of all trial objectives:

    1. Run N trials with a trained model → distribution of objective values
    2. Run M trials with a random baseline → distribution of objective values
    3. Compare the two distributions statistically

    This approach uses all trials, not just the best, avoiding the "data snooping" bias that inflates the apparent performance of the best model selected from many attempts.

    Why this matters: If you run 1,000 trials and pick the best one, that best trial's performance is upward-biased by the number of trials attempted. By comparing entire distributions, the statistical test accounts for the full landscape of model behavior, not just the cherry-picked peak.

    11.2 Statistical Tests

    For each comparison pair (trained model vs random baseline):

    Test What It Measures Requirement
    Mann-Whitney U test Whether the trained model distribution is stochastically greater than the random distribution Non-parametric, doesn't assume normality
    Effect size (rank-biserial correlation or Cohen's d) Practical magnitude of the difference Minimum: > 0.3 (small-medium effect)
    Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals Uncertainty range on the median difference Must not include zero
    Multiple comparison correction (Bonferroni or Holm-Sidak) Corrects for running many tests Applied across all comparison pairs

    Minimum bar for claiming statistical significance:

    • p < 0.05 after correction
    • Effect size > 0.3 (at least small-medium practical effect)
    • Bootstrap CI does not include zero

    11.3 Comparison Pairs

    Each tier has 24 comparison pairs: 6 real models × 4 random baseline types.

    All pairs share the exact same data, features, and scoring pipeline. Each random baseline type runs as its own experiment with 40 trials (different random seeds), enabling per-type Mann-Whitney U comparisons.

    Comparison What It Tests
    Model X vs always_none Is X better than doing nothing?
    Model X vs random_l Is X better than random guessing matching label distribution?
    Model X vs random_b Is X better than random entry timing respecting entry policy?
    Model X vs random_markov Is X better than random predictions with temporal label structure?

    Why separate random experiments: Each random type represents a different null hypothesis. random_markov should be the strongest (captures temporal label structure) and hardest to beat. Per-type comparison reveals which null hypothesis is most challenging.

    Per tier: 24 pairs. Total across 5 tiers: 120 pairs (before multiple comparison correction).

    Applied to date (Tiers A + B): The statistical validation (Section 12) tested 7 model types (6 trainable + rule_based) against 3 random baselines (RLA, RBO, RMK) across 2 tiers (A: 2 features, B: 45 features), 4 temporal folds, and 4 runs. This produced 255 pairwise comparisons and 5 grouped analysis configurations (By Model, By Tier, By Tier×Model, By Model×Fold, By Run×Tier×Model). always_none was used as a null reference but excluded from statistical comparisons (it produces a single deterministic trial, not a testable distribution). Tiers C, D, and E remain pending — their completion will activate the full 120-pair framework described above.

    11.4 PASS/FAIL Criteria

    For a trained model to be declared statistically better than a random baseline:

    1. Real model median objective > random baseline median objective
    2. Mann-Whitney U p-value < 0.05 (after correction)
    3. Effect size > 0.3 (at least small practical effect)

    All three conditions must be satisfied simultaneously.

    11.5 Progressive Complexity Question

    For each tier, models are ranked by median objective. The key question is:

    What is the SIMPLEST model that beats random with statistical significance?

    • If logistic beats random → patterns are linear
    • If only MLP beats random → patterns are nonlinear but don't require sequence modeling
    • If only TCN/VIT beat random → patterns require temporal or attention mechanisms
    • If no model beats random → no extractable patterns exist at that complexity level

    This progressive "ablation" structure is what makes the thesis design powerful: it doesn't just prove "some model works" — it identifies the minimum architectural complexity required.

    11.6 Correction for Multiple Comparisons

    With 120 comparison pairs across 5 tiers, the probability of at least one false positive at α=0.05 without correction is extremely high (~99.8%).

    Correction methods:

    • Bonferroni: Divide α by number of tests. Conservative but simple.
    • Holm-Sidak (preferred): Step-down procedure. Less conservative than Bonferroni while still controlling family-wise error rate.

    Both are applied across all valid pairs within a tier or across all tiers, depending on the claim being made.

    11.7 Advanced Methods: Correcting for Search Bias

    Beyond basic statistical testing, the thesis methodology incorporates advanced methods from quantitative finance literature:

    Deflated Sharpe Ratio (Bailey & López de Prado, 2014):

    • If Sharpe ratio is used as a headline metric, it must be "deflated" to account for the number of trials attempted
    • DSR reduces the observed Sharpe ratio based on the number of trials, non-normality (skewness and kurtosis), and correlation between trials
    • A Sharpe ratio of 1.5 from 1,000 trials is worth much less than a Sharpe of 1.5 from 10 trials

    White's Reality Check (White, 2000):

    • Tests whether the best model is significantly better than a benchmark after accounting for data snooping
    • Uses bootstrap resampling of the entire model universe to construct the null distribution
    • If 100 models are tested and the best has Sharpe = 2.0, White's RC tests whether this value is unusual given that 100 models were tested

    Hansen's Superior Predictive Ability (SPA) Test (Hansen, 2005):

    • More powerful variant of White's Reality Check
    • Tests whether ANY model is superior to the benchmark
    • Handles dependent test statistics and has better power properties

    Romano & Wolf Stepwise Testing (2005):

    • Extension that identifies ALL models that are significantly better than the benchmark (not just whether at least one is)
    • Controls the familywise error rate while maintaining power
    • Useful when multiple models may be genuinely superior

    Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO):

    • Estimates the probability that the best in-sample model will underperform out-of-sample
    • Uses combinatorial cross-validation across multiple data partitions
    • PBO > 0.5 suggests the selection process is likely overfitted

    Status of advanced methods: DSR, White's RC, Hansen's SPA, PBO, and Romano-Wolf have not been applied yet. Current validation (Section 12) uses Mann-Whitney U + Holm-Sidak correction + Bootstrap 95% CI + rank-biserial effect size — which is already a rigorous non-parametric framework. The advanced methods are scheduled for deployment as the tier count increases from 2 to 5, and the multiple-comparison burden grows from 255 to ~600+ pairwise tests.

    11.8 Why This Methodology Matters

    The average vs best distinction is critical:

    Approach What It Proves Selection Bias Risk
    Best model vs best random "I found one trial that looks good" High — cherry-picking from many attempts
    Average model vs average random "Models are better ON AVERAGE" Low — uses full distribution
    Distribution comparison (Mann-Whitney U) "The entire distribution of model performance exceeds the random distribution" Lowest — the gold standard

    By comparing distributions, the thesis answers: "Does training a model reliably produce better outcomes than random, across the entire landscape of hyperparameter configurations?" — not merely "Can I find at least one lucky configuration?"

    13. Preliminary Thesis Conclusion

    13.1 What The Evidence Supports

    1. Trained models learn transferable signal that randoms cannot produce.

    • Evidence: S2 range of 0.24–0.34 for trained models vs 0.19–0.25 for randoms. The gap exists across all model architectures and all folds. This is not noise — it is systematic.

    2. The learned signal does not automatically convert to economic profit.

    • Evidence: r(S2, PnL) = -0.01 globally. Only 1.2% of trials are profitable. The friction paradox dominates.

    3. Profitability is governed by selectivity, not signal quality.

    • Evidence: r(Trades, PnL) = -0.81. All 14 profitable trials have ≤228 trades. Profitable trials average 106 trades vs 239 for losers.

    4. Some model architectures are better than others.

    • Evidence: VIT (3% profitable) and TCN (2% profitable) outperform MLP (1%), while MEX, AXV, and LOG produce 0% profitable trials. TCN achieves the best single-trial result (Obj=0.543, Sharpe=3.58). VIT is the most consistent across folds.

    5. The market regime (fold) matters.

    • Evidence: F1 and F3 produce all 14 profitable trials. F2 and F4 produce zero. This could reflect different market regimes across the 4 temporal windows.

    13.2 What The Evidence Does NOT Support (Yet)

    1. That models reliably beat random baselines in economic terms.

    • Current status: The formal Mann-Whitney U tests have now been executed across Tiers A and B (see Section 12 and the Statistical Tests Annexo). Trained models statistically outperform randoms on objective_value (5/7 architectures pass, p < 0.05 corrected). However, both distributions still produce negative average PnL — statistical superiority in signal quality does not yet translate to economic profitability.

    2. That adding features improves outcomes.

    • Current status: 45 features buy +0.019 S2 at the cost of +0.228 overfitting gap and -$122 PnL on average. The net effect is negative to neutral.

    3. That any specific architecture has "solved" the problem.

    • Current status: Even VIT (best profitability rate at 3%) still loses money 97% of the time.

    13.3 Open Questions for Subsequent Tiers

    1. Can selectivity be engineered? — The rescue design must explicitly control trade count (100–150 target zone). Threshold tuning showed promise in the extra backtests addendum: 48/60 reruns improved net PnL by trading less.

    2. Does multi-symbol generalization help or hurt? — Tiers C/D/E introduce multi-symbol data. If the signal is real, it should generalize across symbols. If it doesn't, the signal may be overfitted to BTCUSDT.

    14.2 Model Ensembles: Consensus as a Selectivity Filter

    The friction paradox (Section 4) shows that profitability is governed by trade count, not signal quality. An ensemble that requires multiple models to agree before entering a trade is the most direct research-backed attack on this bottleneck.

    Approach Mechanism Research Backing
    Average-probability ensemble (top-K trials per fold) Noise reduction via averaging idiosyncratic trial variance Fawaz et al., 2020 — InceptionTime ensemble is the standard benchmark for time-series classification
    Unanimity/majority-vote ensemble Consensus reduces false positives → fewer trades with higher conviction Standard ensemble theory; directly addresses r(Trades,PnL) = -0.81
    Cross-architecture ensemble (MLP + TCN + VIT) Different architectures capture different pattern types Gu, Kelly & Xiu, RFS 2020 — ML ensemble for asset pricing shows economic gains from combining model families

    Why this matters for the thesis: Our data already shows that TCN, VIT, and MLP_EX capture different aspects of the signal (Section 8). A unanimity-vote ensemble would trade only when architectures with different inductive biases agree — naturally filtering marginal trades. The Ensemble Implementation Plan specifies a concrete 7-milestone implementation path.

    14.5 Market Regime Detection and Regime-Aware Trading

    The Regime Audit showed that model performance is strongly regime-conditional: models perform differently in trending vs ranging vs volatile markets. Currently, regime information enters the model as 6 binary signals (e.g., ADX ≥ 25 → trend), which destroys gradient information.

    Improvement Current State Proposed Research Backing
    Continuous regime features 6 binary signals (0/1) 5 continuous + 5 persistence + 2 meta = 12 columns MARKET_STATE Plan — replaces information-destroying discretization
    Regime persistence Not tracked bars_since_change, regime_stability, transition_recency Regime audit: models break during transitions
    HMM regime identification Not implemented 2-3 state latent model (bull/bear/choppy) Crypto regime-switching papers: 2023 HMM; 2026 Markov-switching volatility

    Why this matters: The per-fold analysis (Section 9) shows that F1 and F3 produce all profitable trials while F2 and F4 produce zero — strong evidence that profitability is regime-dependent. Training regime-aware models (or gating execution by regime) directly attacks this pattern. The MARKET_STATE feature plan is already designed and ready to implement.

    14.8 The Improvement Multiplication Effect

    Each improvement avenue addresses a different bottleneck in the current system. Their effects are expected to be at least partially independent and therefore multiplicative, not merely additive:

    Current Bottleneck Improvement Avenue Addresses
    Too many trades (friction) Ensembles (14.2), Locked levers (14.6) Selectivity → fewer, higher-conviction trades
    Limited information (price only) Alt data (14.3), Multi-TF/symbol (14.4) Feature space → more signal sources
    Regime blindness Regime detection (14.5) Context → trade only when conditions favor the model
    Architecture ceiling New architectures (14.7) Representation → better signal extraction

    Quantitative perspective on the potential:

    • Feature expansion: Moving from 2 to 45 features increased effect sizes by 53% (Section 12.4). Moving from 45 to 355 features (Tier E) represents a 7.9× expansion — the literature suggests diminishing but still positive returns.
    • Threshold tuning: The backtests annex showed 80% of reruns improved PnL by simply reducing trades. This lever is currently locked.
    • Ensemble filtering: If a 3-architecture unanimity vote reduces trades by 50–70%, the friction paradox analysis predicts a $200–$400 PnL improvement per trial at current friction rates.
    • Regime gating: If models trade only in 2 of 4 regimes (the profitable ones suggested by F1/F3 performance), they avoid the regimes that produce 0% profitability.

    The compound effect: None of these improvements have been applied yet. Each is backed by peer-reviewed research. Each addresses a different failure mode. The first two tiers establish that the foundation exists — a real, statistically validated signal. The remaining tiers and post-thesis optimization phases are designed to amplify that signal through these documented, evidence-based pathways.

    Bottom line: What we have demonstrated is a small but real and statistically validated edge in a domain where most published approaches fail to beat calibrated random baselines at all. The improvement potential is not speculative — it is mapped, designed, and backed by literature. The path from 1.2% profitability rate to a viable system runs through the concrete avenues documented above.
