    Model options for small-window multi-timeframe trading sequences

    Framing your current setup and the core modeling problem

    Your current “TradingViT” is a Transformer encoder that consumes a 2D time-series window shaped as [batch, seq_len, features], projects features to an embedding dimension, adds a learned positional embedding, runs a stack of self-attention layers, then pools across time and produces multi-head outputs (class + two bounded regressions). This is structurally closer to a standard Transformer encoder from “Attention Is All You Need” than to a classical vision ViT that forms 2D patches from an image grid. citeturn11search0turn11search1

    From the perspective of research literature, your input is a multivariate time series window (MTS). The central question is how to (a) encode temporal order, (b) model cross-feature interactions, and (c) stay stable under small-window supervision and non-stationary market regimes. The Transformer family is attractive because self-attention can connect any two positions in a sequence. citeturn11search0turn13search11

    Two details in your setting matter for model choice:

    First, you treat the window as “ticks” where a tick can mean 1h bars or 1d bars, and features can themselves come from multiple timeframes aligned by timestamp. This creates a representation where position index is a lag counter, but the real elapsed time per step can differ by feature/timeframe. In the broader time-series literature this resembles misaligned or irregular sampling across variables, a case where adding explicit time-interval information or adopting continuous-time models can change results. citeturn6search2turn6search0turn3search3

    Second, your pipeline evaluates with temporal splits and embargo-like logic. This matches a known issue in finance: naive random CV can leak future-dependent information. A finance-specific discussion of purging/embargo and time-aware validation is covered in entity["book","Advances in Financial Machine Learning","lopez de prado 2018"] and related material. citeturn14search1turn14search14

    Transformer-family time-series backbones worth testing

    A useful way to organize Transformer alternatives is by what they tokenize and how they inject inductive bias for time.

    Patch tokens instead of timestamp tokens

    Patch-based tokenization adapts the key ViT idea (“tokens are patches”) from vision to time series. In ViT, an image is split into fixed patches and fed to a Transformer. citeturn11search1

    For time series, PatchTST applies segmentation along the time axis into subseries “patches” that become tokens, often with a “channel-independence” design. This is motivated by long lookbacks, but patching can also help short windows by forcing a local aggregation prior to attention, which can act as a bias toward local patterns. citeturn0search0turn13search7

    How this maps to your use: keep your multi-head outputs, replace the encoder with “patch embed → transformer over patches → pool → heads”. When seq_len=50, you can test patch sizes like 5 or 10 (tokens 10 or 5), which changes attention’s effective granularity. The PatchTST paper and the broader Transformer-in-time-series survey both describe patching as a response to limitations of timestamp-level tokenization. citeturn0search0turn13search7turn13search11

    Variable tokens instead of time tokens

    A dominant failure mode for multivariate time series is collapsing many variables at each timestep into a single token embedding, which can blur variable-specific dynamics. iTransformer flips the dimensions: it embeds time points of each variable into “variate tokens” and applies attention across variables, while feed-forward layers operate per variable token. citeturn0search9turn0search1

    For your setting (many features and a modest seq_len), a “variables-as-tokens” backbone can be a direct fit: each feature becomes one token, and each token contains the lag vector of length seq_len (embedded by a small MLP/1D conv). This also aligns with your “2D matrix” intuition: you are now tokenizing along the feature axis instead of the time axis. The iTransformer paper provides the rationale and empirical results in forecasting contexts; the architectural idea transfers to classification/regression heads. citeturn0search9turn13search11

    Explicit cross-dimension modeling

    Crossformer targets multivariate time series by embedding the input into a 2D array and modeling cross-dimension dependency explicitly, rather than treating cross-variable interaction as a side effect of time-token embeddings. citeturn4search0turn4search4

    This design direction is relevant when your signal depends on interactions between indicators (feature-feature) as much as on temporal patterns (time-time). It is also aligned with your idea of the window as a 2D object rather than a 1D sequence. citeturn4search0turn13search11

    Time-series Transformers with built-in priors (decomposition, periodicity, multi-scale)

    Several time-series Transformers add structural priors. Autoformer integrates decomposition (trend/seasonal) and an autocorrelation mechanism as a replacement for standard self-attention in long-term forecasting. citeturn1search1turn1search5
    FEDformer uses seasonal-trend decomposition and frequency-domain mechanisms for long horizons. citeturn1search2turn1search6
    TimesNet models temporal variation in a 2D space with an emphasis on multi-periodicity and is presented as “task-general” across time-series tasks. citeturn1search15turn1search3
    Multi-resolution Transformer lines also formalize multi-scale processing and cite patching as a mechanism derived from ViT-style tokenization. citeturn9search9turn11search1

    For your use, these families matter less for computational scaling (50 steps is not long) and more for inductive bias: decomposition and periodicity priors can stabilize training when labels are sparse/noisy or when regime drift is present. citeturn13search11turn14search16

    Time-aware position encoding variants

    Transformers do not encode order by default; they rely on positional information additions or changes to attention. citeturn11search0turn11search7
    For time series classification, ConvTran specifically focuses on absolute and relative position encoding variants (tAPE + eRPE) and couples them with a convolution-based input encoding for multivariate time series classification. citeturn10search13

    In your case, this is one of the most “surgical” transformer-family changes: keep your encoder stack, replace absolute learned position_embedding with a relative or hybrid scheme, and compare outcomes under different window sizes and multiple tick granularities. citeturn10search13turn11search7

    Non-Transformer baselines that often match or beat attention on short windows

    A recurring pattern in time-series research is that model class matters less than bias/regularization and evaluation protocol, and that non-attention models can dominate on many tasks and datasets.

    Temporal convolutional networks and Inception-style CNNs

    Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs) use dilated causal convolutions to build receptive fields across time without recurrence. A systematic evaluation by Bai et al. reports that a generic convolutional architecture can outperform LSTMs across multiple sequence modeling tasks and can have longer effective memory. citeturn2search0turn13search0

    For time-series classification, InceptionTime is a CNN ensemble inspired by Inception modules and is evaluated on standard time-series classification benchmarks, with claims about scalability compared to prior methods. citeturn0search3turn0search7

    Why these belong in your shortlist: for seq_len≈50, convolution kernels with dilation or multi-scale filters match the “small windows” regime, and they impose a local pattern bias that can be useful in noisy financial data contexts. citeturn2search0turn0search7turn14search16

    ROCKET and MiniROCKET as “fast strong baselines” for window classification

    ROCKET transforms a time series with many random convolutional kernels and trains a linear classifier on summary features. MiniROCKET reformulates ROCKET to reduce compute and increase determinism while maintaining reported accuracy levels on UCR benchmarks. citeturn7search0turn7search1turn7search4

    These methods are not neural sequence models in the Transformer sense, but they are relevant because your core directional label is a discrete classification problem on fixed-length windows. Using them as baselines can clarify whether the expensive part of modeling is temporal pattern extraction or downstream decision calibration. citeturn7search0turn7search1

    Linear / MLP mixers as control points

    The paper “Are Transformers Effective for Time Series Forecasting?” argues that self-attention is permutation-invariant and can lose temporal information, and shows that simple linear baselines (LTSF-Linear) outperform multiple Transformer variants on long-term forecasting benchmarks. citeturn5search0turn5search12
    Related follow-on work includes Mixer-style architectures like TSMixer that alternate mixing across time and feature dimensions using MLP blocks. citeturn4search1turn4search17

    Even if you are not doing forecasting, this line of results supports including at least one “no-attention” baseline: flatten+MLP, TCN, or a time-feature mixer. citeturn5search0turn4search1

    Sequence backbones beyond Transformers

    Structured state space models and Mamba-family models

    Structured State Space models (S4) are proposed as sequence models derived from continuous-time state space formulations, with attention to computational feasibility and performance on long-range dependency benchmarks. citeturn3search0turn3search4
    Mamba proposes selective state space models as a linear-time alternative backbone to attention and reports scaling to long sequences and throughput differences versus Transformers. citeturn3search1turn3search5turn13search6

    For your “small window” regime, SSMs are not about enabling long context, but about providing an alternate inductive bias for temporal dynamics with different optimization behavior than attention. If your current Transformer shows collapse, unstable training, or sensitivity to positional encoding choices, SSM backbones can be a controlled experiment. citeturn3search0turn3search1turn10search13

    Continuous-time models for irregular or misaligned sampling

    When observations are not aligned on a single uniform grid, approaches that embed time intervals explicitly or treat hidden state dynamics in continuous time become relevant.

    GRU-D incorporates missingness masks and time intervals into a recurrent model for multivariate time series with missing values. citeturn6search2
    Latent ODEs and ODE-RNNs define continuous-time hidden dynamics and can operate across arbitrary time gaps. citeturn6search1turn6search5
    Neural Controlled Differential Equations (Neural CDEs) target irregularly sampled multivariate time series and report results against ODE/RNN baselines. citeturn6search0turn6search4
    Time2Vec proposes a model-agnostic vector representation of time that can be used in many architectures. citeturn3search3

    In your pipeline, the multi-timeframe alignment “by last timestamp ≤ t” can create effective irregularity across features when interpreted in real elapsed time. If you choose to represent that explicitly (time deltas per feature per lag), these model classes become more directly applicable. citeturn6search2turn6search1turn3search3

    Multi-timeframe representation strategies that match your window construction

    Your current window is a 2D object (lags × features), but features may come from different timeframes and “lag index” does not correspond to a single shared Δt across features. The literature suggests several ways to address similar structure.

    One approach is explicit time encoding: append time delta features or learn time embeddings. Time2Vec supports this as a drop-in time representation that can be combined with attention, convolutions, or MLPs. citeturn3search3
    If you stay within the Transformer family, ConvTran’s focus on absolute/relative position and its multivariate classification framing make it relevant to this axis. citeturn10search13

    A second approach is architectural separation by resolution: encode each timeframe group with its own encoder, then fuse. The multi-resolution Transformer paper is an example of encoding and fusing multiple resolutions in a formal way, albeit in forecasting contexts. citeturn9search9
    TimesNet’s multi-periodicity framing also fits multi-timeframe mixtures, because multiple periodicities show up as multiple dominant scales. citeturn1search15turn1search3

    A third approach is to change tokenization: iTransformer’s variable tokens or Crossformer’s cross-dimension design both push modeling capacity toward feature-feature structure, which can be useful when the “time axis” is partly a bookkeeping device for multiple time bases. citeturn0search9turn4search0

    Updated research directions for pretraining and transfer to your supervised heads

    Small labeled datasets and regime drift motivate pretraining and transfer.

    A Transformer-based representation learning framework for multivariate time series is presented by Zerveas et al., with an explicit goal of pretraining for downstream tasks including classification and regression. citeturn10search0turn10search8
    TS2Vec is an unsupervised contrastive framework that learns timestamp-level representations and reports improvements on time-series classification benchmarks. citeturn5search1turn5search9
    A survey on self-supervised learning for time series proposes a taxonomy across generative, contrastive, and adversarial families, which helps in selecting pretraining objectives. citeturn10search6turn10search14

    On the foundation model side, recent work focuses on large-scale pretraining for forecasting. TimesFM is released by entity["company","Google","tech company"] Research as a decoder-only foundation model for time-series forecasting, with claims about zero-shot transfer across domains and granularities. citeturn5search2turn5search10turn5search6
    Chronos is released by entity["company","Amazon","e-commerce and cloud"] as a pretrained probabilistic forecasting framework that tokenizes time series values and trains language-model-like architectures. citeturn5search3turn5search15
    TimeGPT is proposed by entity["company","Nixtla","time series company"] as a foundation model for time series with reported zero-shot forecasting results. citeturn8search3turn8search7
    Timer proposes GPT-style generative pretraining for time series and frames it as “large time series models.” citeturn10search3turn8search10

    These models are trained for forecasting, not your label scheme, but they point to a transfer pattern: use pretrained encoders/embeddings and train your classification/regression heads on top, or fine-tune end-to-end if data and compute allow. citeturn10search0turn5search6turn5search3turn10search3

    Model shortlist and experimental matrix aligned with your pipeline

    A practical selection strategy is to test across three axes: tokenization, temporal-order mechanism, and cross-feature mechanism, while keeping your heads and your Stage1/2/3 evaluation stable.

    A minimal shortlist that spans the space:

    A “convolution-first” backbone: TCN or InceptionTime-style CNN (plus your three heads). citeturn2search0turn0search7
    A “Transformer with position focus” backbone: ConvTran-style position encoding + conv embedding + attention stack. citeturn10search13
    A “patch-token” Transformer: PatchTST-like patch embed + Transformer. citeturn0search0turn13search7
    A “feature-token” Transformer: iTransformer-like inversion (features as tokens, lag vectors embedded per feature). citeturn0search9turn0search1
    A non-attention control: ROCKET/MiniROCKET or an MLP mixer. citeturn7search0turn7search1turn4search1
    A non-Transformer sequence backbone: S4 or Mamba-style SSM blocks with your heads. citeturn3search0turn3search1

    For your multi-timeframe setting, add one explicit test per model: with and without time-interval/timeframe encoding. Time2Vec is one method for time representation; continuous-time models (Neural CDE / Latent ODE) are another path if you make the time axis explicit rather than implicit. citeturn3search3turn6search0turn6search1

    For evaluation, keep two checks separate:

    Offline predictive checks: calibration of tradeability probabilities and regression head error conditional on tradeable labels. This follows from your pipeline design and aligns with multi-head supervision.
    Backtest checks: Stage3 behavior under entry gating and thresholds. In finance literature, regime drift and non-stationarity can reverse offline improvements, so stage-by-stage stability is a required filter. citeturn14search16turn14search2
