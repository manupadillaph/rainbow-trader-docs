    What your codebase is already set up to prove

    Your repositories already encode a falsification-style thesis test: separate “patterned,” “pattern-free,” and “real-market” data sources; run both real models and calibrated random baselines through the same pipeline; and optionally inject controlled leakage to verify the end-to-end flow and measure how much “cheating” changes results. fileciteturn32file0L1-L1

    At the experiment level, your core config object explicitly treats “data provider” as a first-class lever (ExperimentConfig.data_provider) alongside the base symbol/timeframe, global range, feature window definition, Optuna settings, scoring settings, and a progressive “phases” system (PhasesConfig). fileciteturn32file0L1-L1

    Your repo includes ready-to-run experiment templates that operationalize your “synthetic controls” idea:

    • A dummy experiment template (data_provider: "dummy", symbol DUMMYUSDT, 1h) that uses a small feature set (close + RSI) and a bounded Optuna search space to keep runs tractable. fileciteturn41file1L1-L1
    • A random experiment template (data_provider: "random", symbol RANDOMUSDT, 1h) that mirrors the dummy setup but is intended to be patternless under the null, again with a small Optuna search space. fileciteturn43file0L1-L1

    At the configuration layer, you also define DummyProviderConfig as a synthetic sine-wave generator (amplitude / phase / period in candles) and RandomProviderConfig as a seeded random-walk style generator with configurable “jump” distribution. fileciteturn8file0L1-L1

    A dedicated “dummy pipeline” script in rainbow-trader shows the intended full pipeline order (sync data → process features → create experiment → generate labels → build windows → train → backtest) and is designed to let you swap providers via config overrides. fileciteturn41file0L1-L1

    Crucially, you already implemented two “controlled leakage / controlled shortcut” features that map directly to your thesis validation narrative:

    • A LEAK indicator that explicitly introduces temporal leakage by shifting an input series backward (bringing future values into the present), for sanity-checks and leak-sensitivity experiments. fileciteturn29file0L1-L1
    • A label-copy signal that recomputes LONG/SHORT labels using the same labeling algorithm as your labeling service, then exposes them as binary feature columns—effectively a “cheat code” feature to prove models can recover an extremely direct signal when it exists. fileciteturn29file19L1-L1

    On the modeling side, you built a family of random baselines intended to be run through the exact same training/backtesting machinery as the real networks. The model-type enum includes both real models (e.g., ViT variants) and random baselines (always-flat, label-prior matched, entry-policy matched, Markov). fileciteturn23file6L1-L1 That “random-but-calibrated” design is very important for thesis-grade inference: it makes the null hypothesis stronger than naïve coin-flips, reducing the chance you “win” because a model merely exploits class imbalance or trivial persistence rather than learning market structure.

    Finally, your stack already centralizes “what counts as better” into the Optuna scoring layer: you translate backtest metrics (e.g., profitability, drawdown, Sharpe, win rate) into normalized stage scores and a weighted composite objective for trial comparison. fileciteturn16file4L1-L1

    Market efficiency and why your thesis is not automatically anti-EMH

    The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) is often summarized as “markets are unpredictable,” but the stronger claim is about whether abnormal risk-adjusted profits are possible once information is public and trading costs/competition are considered. The canonical survey formalized the “weak,” “semi-strong,” and “strong” forms and framed the empirical problem as joint tests of market efficiency and an asset-pricing model. citeturn0search2

    A key nuance for your thesis write-up is that “evidence against a pure random walk” is not identical to “guaranteed exploitable edge at scale.” In fact, classic tests reject the random-walk hypothesis in equity data, for example through variance-ratio style tests, without necessarily implying that a specific trading rule will survive costs, market impact, and competition. citeturn2search0turn2search4

    Your thesis framing fits well with the Adaptive Markets Hypothesis (AMH): markets can look efficient in some regimes and less efficient in others because the ecology of participants, constraints, and available technology changes over time. AMH explicitly reframes “efficiency” as evolving rather than fixed, which matches your intuition about regime dependency and why a system should be evaluated across multiple market environments. citeturn0search1

    Empirical finance also documents persistent return patterns (often treated as “anomalies”) such as momentum—strategies that buy recent winners and sell recent losers generating positive average returns in certain horizons—again with caveats around implementation realism and robustness over time. citeturn2search5

    Machine learning and deep learning in finance, with emphasis on transformers and ViT

    A large, credible stream of ML-in-finance evidence focuses on the cross-section of expected returns (forecasting relative returns across many assets rather than directionally trading a single series). A flagship result is the “Empirical Asset Pricing via Machine Learning” work by entity["people","Shihao Gu","finance researcher"], entity["people","Bryan Kelly","finance professor"], and entity["people","Dacheng Xiu","finance professor"], which benchmarks many ML methods and reports meaningful economic gains in portfolio implementations relative to traditional linear models, attributing gains to nonlinear interactions among predictors. citeturn4search4

    A second stream is market microstructure and ultra-short-horizon predictability, where the “signal” (if any) is often tied to order flow and limit order book dynamics rather than daily OHLCV. DeepLOB is a widely cited deep learning approach for mid-price movement prediction from limit order book data, and is frequently discussed as evidence that deep networks can extract stable patterns from certain microstructure-rich datasets. citeturn4search2

    Why transformers are attractive for your codebase’s representation

    Transformers were introduced as a sequence model that replaces recurrence with self-attention, enabling direct modeling of long-range dependencies. citeturn9search1 For multivariate time series, transformer variants are commonly motivated by the need to learn (1) which features matter and (2) which time points matter, often under nonstationarity.

    Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) by entity["people","Bryan Lim","machine learning researcher"] and coauthors is particularly relevant as a “bridge citation” for your thesis narrative because it is an attention-based architecture explicitly designed for multi-horizon forecasting with mixed inputs and includes mechanisms for variable selection and interpretability. citeturn9search8

    Finance-specific transformer applications are growing and increasingly appear in venues beyond arbitrary preprints. For example: - A 2024 entity["company","IBM","technology company"] research publication presents a multimodal transformer for financial time series forecasting that explicitly targets combining text and numeric time series sources. citeturn6search7
    - An open-access entity["organization","Expert Systems with Applications","journal, elsevier"] paper applies transformer architectures with time embeddings to FX-spot prediction and includes benchmarking against alternatives, reflecting ongoing empirical exploration of transformer suitability in financial forecasting problems. citeturn6search9

    Vision Transformer and the “charts contain information” angle

    Vision Transformer (ViT) formalizes the idea that an image can be represented as a sequence of patches (tokens) and processed with transformer blocks, achieving strong results when pre-trained at scale. citeturn8search3

    This matters for your implementation because your “FAT window” representation is structurally similar to a 2D grid (feature × time) and your model space explicitly includes ViT-style architectures, indicating you are treating the window as a visually structured object rather than only as a 1D sequence. fileciteturn23file6L1-L1

    More importantly for thesis defense, there is now peer-reviewed evidence (not just blog posts) that visual information in stock charts can contain economically meaningful signals. A 2026 entity["organization","Finance Research Letters","journal, elsevier"] paper (“From vision to value…”) reports that factors derived from deep learning on stock chart images—including ViT-derived signals—can add incremental pricing power beyond a large set of established factors, which is very closely aligned with your high-level claim “there are patterns in the numbers and models can extract them.” citeturn7search8

    In addition, entity["organization","IEEE Access","journal, ieee"] published work (2025) explicitly combining ViT-like processing of candlestick image representations with transformer-style temporal models for movement classification, including statistical comparisons to baselines. citeturn7search1turn7search5

    Statistical significance and multiple-testing controls are the core thesis risk

    If your system runs “thousands of combinations” of features, windows, and hyperparameters, the main scientific challenge becomes data snooping / multiple testing: discovering a configuration that looks excellent by chance in a finite historical sample. The classic formalization is White’s Reality Check, which proposes a bootstrap-based test of whether the best model in a search has predictive superiority over a benchmark once you account for the specification search itself. citeturn0search0

    Hansen’s Superior Predictive Ability (SPA) test addresses a related problem: testing whether a benchmark is outperformed by at least one alternative while reducing sensitivity to poor alternatives and data snooping effects. citeturn1search0

    entity["people","Joseph P. Romano","econometrician"] and entity["people","Michael Wolf","econometrician"] extend this theme with stepwise multiple testing procedures aimed at controlling family-wise error rates in the context of comparing many strategies to a common benchmark (exactly the “many trials” situation your Optuna + feature-selection search creates). citeturn2search1

    In mainstream asset-pricing research, the “factor zoo” critique provides a widely cited argument that the usual t>2 significance rule becomes too weak after decades of mining. entity["people","Campbell R. Harvey","finance economist"], entity["people","Yan Liu","finance researcher"], and entity["people","Heqing Zhu","finance researcher"] propose a multiple-testing framework and argue that new discoveries should clear a substantially higher t-statistic hurdle (often summarized as “around 3.0” rather than 2.0) to reduce false discoveries. citeturn3search2

    For performance metrics, you should be especially careful with Sharpe ratios. The “Deflated Sharpe Ratio” work explicitly targets selection bias and non-normality, providing a correction framework for “best-of-many backtests.” citeturn1search2 This connects directly to your “compare against random models repeatedly” plan: you are effectively constructing an empirical distribution of baseline outcomes, and deflation-style corrections are designed for this setting.

    Relatedly, the “Probability of Backtest Overfitting” line of work formalizes how quickly apparent backtest skill can be manufactured when many configurations are tried, and proposes cross-validation approaches to estimate overfitting risk. citeturn1search9turn1search7

    Random baselines and “null experiments” as a principled scientific method

    Your setup (dummy vs random provider, real models vs random baselines, with optional leakage) is best understood as a three-layer inference strategy:

    1. Positive control: On deterministic synthetic data (sine-wave-like dynamics), a competent pipeline should learn and profit (or at least achieve high predictive scores). Your dummy provider and dummy template are built for that. fileciteturn8file0L1-L1
    2. Negative control: On random data, performance should collapse to baseline unless leakage is introduced. Your random provider template is built for this. fileciteturn43file0L1-L1
    3. Mechanism check: Introduce known leakage and confirm performance increases, which demonstrates the pipeline is sensitive to information flow and that your evaluation can detect “cheating.” Your LEAK indicator and label-copy signal are explicit mechanisms for this. fileciteturn29file0L1-L1 fileciteturn29file19L1-L1

    Statistically, what you are doing aligns with the null-hypothesis framing behind Reality Check / SPA: you define a benchmark (random baseline or no-skill model) and test whether a searched-over family of strategies beats it after adjusting for search. citeturn0search0turn1search0

    Your random-model family is a particularly strong benchmark because some variants are calibrated from the observed label distribution and transition behavior (e.g., Markov structure), which means the baseline can mimic “class prior exploitation” and simple persistence patterns. That is exactly what a skeptic would demand if you claim you found true predictive structure. fileciteturn23file2L1-L1 fileciteturn23file7L1-L1

    In addition to formal tests (Reality Check / SPA), the broader inference toolkit for time-series strategy evaluation often uses resampling. White’s Reality Check is itself bootstrap-based, and the literature commonly emphasizes that resampling should respect serial dependence (e.g., block/bootstrap variants rather than iid shuffles) when dealing with time series. citeturn0search0

    Data leakage, temporal alignment, and why your “leak toggles” are valuable

    In financial ML, leakage often enters through subtle time alignment mistakes (feature computed using future prices, target not shifted correctly, normalization using full-sample statistics, etc.). The consequence is exactly what your prompt described: a system appears to “find patterns” in truly random data. White’s discussion of data snooping makes the general point that repeated reuse of a single history can create convincing but spurious results without any real underlying merit. citeturn0search0

    Your code includes two extremely useful, explicit leakage probes:

    • The LEAK indicator is declared as intentionally leaking (“shifting backward”), providing a “known-bad” feature to confirm end-to-end leak sensitivity and to measure how much such a shift inflates scores. fileciteturn29file0L1-L1
    • The label-copy signal literally recomputes the label logic and exposes the resulting LONG/SHORT as features, creating an almost direct path from future-labeled outcomes into the model input (a controlled “shortcut learning” test). fileciteturn29file19L1-L1

    These are also conceptually aligned with modern leakage discussions: a controlled “break the pipeline on purpose” test is one of the fastest ways to verify that a training/backtest stack reacts strongly to future information and therefore must be guarded carefully in the real experiments. citeturn10search4

    Note on the “Kaufman (2020)” reference from your prompt: I did not find a clearly identifiable, primary academic source matching that exact citation that is specifically about temporal leakage in ML backtesting. The name is ambiguous (multiple authors named Kaufman publish on data/analytics). If you have the paper title or venue, it can be pinned down and added as a precise citation in the bibliography.

    On the prevention side, a big part of leakage avoidance is ensuring that window construction uses only data available up to the decision timestamp. Your window builder explicitly locates the last index at or before the sampling timestamp and slices a historical window ending at that time, rather than slicing symmetric windows around it. fileciteturn36file4L1-L1

    Similarly, your labeling strategy is explicitly future-dependent (labels are computed from the next horizon of price moves) which is correct for supervised learning, but also means that training/test splits must avoid overlapping label horizons. The label generation logic is clearly based on forward windows of future increments (max future move up/down and constraints before reaching the extrema). fileciteturn26file0L1-L1

    The backtest-overfitting literature (PBO / related work) frames this as a structural problem: in finance your labels and events have temporal extent, and naive data splitting can accidentally let information bleed across train/test boundaries. citeturn1search9turn1search7

    Curated bibliography mapped to your thesis questions

    This section lists the most thesis-relevant papers/sources from the research scan and explains why each is load-bearing for your defense.

    Efficient markets and “where predictability can exist”: - Fama (1970): defines EMH forms and frames the empirical difficulty (joint tests). Use this to define what claim you are and are not making. citeturn0search2

    - DeepLOB (2018 arXiv; later IEEE TSP): deep learning can extract stable patterns from microstructure data and generalize across instruments, supporting the “patterns exist in some market data” narrative. citeturn4search2
    - entity["people","Ashish Vaswani","computer scientist"] et al. (2017): transformer architecture foundation; cite to justify why attention-based models are reasonable sequence learners. citeturn9search1
    - entity["people","Alexey Dosovitskiy","computer scientist"] et al. (ViT, 2021): patch-token modeling foundation; cite to justify why a 2D “window grid” can plausibly be fed to ViT-like architectures. citeturn8search3
    - Finance Research Letters (2026), “From vision to value…”: direct evidence that ViT on stock chart images can produce signals with incremental pricing power; this is uniquely aligned with your “patterns detectable by models” thesis. citeturn7search8
    - Temporal Fusion Transformer (2019/2021): widely used attention-based forecasting design with variable selection and interpretability; useful to justify systematic feature selection and multi-horizon design decisions. citeturn9search8
    - IBM (ICAIF 2024) and ESWA (2024 FX transformer): modern finance-specific transformer deployments and benchmarking culture. citeturn6search7turn6search9

    Statistical significance, data snooping, and “best-of-many” backtests: - White (2000) Reality Check: the key citation when you search over many configurations and report the best one; directly addresses your Optuna situation. citeturn0search0
    - Hansen (2005) SPA: related superiority test designed for many-model comparisons against a benchmark. citeturn1search0
    - Romano & Wolf (2005): stepwise multiple testing controlling family-wise error rates in benchmarking many strategies. citeturn2search1
    - Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016): “factor zoo” multiple testing framework; use this for the meta-argument “the default statistical bar is too low once you do large searches.” citeturn3search2
    - Bailey & López de Prado (2014) Deflated Sharpe Ratio: performance metric correction for selection bias, overfitting, and non-normality; directly relevant if Sharpe is a key headline metric. citeturn1search2
    - Bailey et al. (2014/2017) Backtest overfitting / PBO: formalizes why trying many variants makes excellent backtests unsurprising and proposes tools to quantify overfitting risk. citeturn1search1turn1search9
